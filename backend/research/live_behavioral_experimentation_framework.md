## 1. Crowd Synchronization in Social Environments

**Mechanism**  
Synchronization emerges through *interpersonal entrainment* — the spontaneous alignment of physiological and motor rhythms across individuals via shared attention and periodic environmental signals (e.g., musical beat). At the neural level, this is driven by *neural entrainment*: endogenous brain oscillations phase-lock to external rhythmic stimuli, particularly in the delta band (0.5–4 Hz), which corresponds to the tactus or “beat” frequency of most music. In social contexts, this is amplified by *interbrain synchrony* (IBS), where co-present individuals exhibit correlated activity in frontotemporal theta networks during shared pleasurable experiences.

**Trigger**
- Live musical performance with salient rhythmic structure (fast-tempo, high-groove music produces stronger phase-locking than slow/rubato passages).
- Physical co-presence within ~1–2 meters; proximity increases affective synchrony and cardiac coupling.
- Shared attention directed toward a focal point (stage, performer, or ritual action).

**Expected Behavioral Effect**
- Audience members nod, sway, or tap in temporal alignment.
- Increased prosocial behavior and perceived social affiliation post-synchronization.
- Enhanced perceived pleasure and engagement with the performance.

**Measurable Indicators**
- *Neural*: EEG delta/theta phase-locking value (PLV) between audience members; cerebro-acoustic phase-locking to note onsets.
- *Physiological*: Cardiac inter-subject correlation (ISC); heart rate variability (HRV) coupling in 0.04–0.15 Hz (LF) band.
- *Motor*: Head-movement synchrony (motion capture); tapping/jerking onset alignment.
- *Proxemic*: Interpersonal distance reduction; orientation toward focal point.

**Possible KPI Candidates**
- Delta-band PLV per zone.
- Percentage of dyads showing HRV time-frequency coherence &gt; threshold.
- Head-nod synchrony index (cross-recurrence quantification).
- Spatial clustering coefficient (how many patrons orient toward stage).

**Contextual Limitations**
- Requires controlled acoustics; phase-locking collapses if SPL varies &gt;3 dB across zones.
- Musical training in audience may inflate neural measures; general populations show weaker but present effects.
- Visual occlusion (eyes closed) reduces but does not eliminate the live-performance synchrony boost.

**Contradictions / Uncertainty**
- Some studies find HRV synchrony without subjective “togetherness” reports, suggesting autonomic coupling can occur without conscious social bonding.
- Fast music strengthens neural entrainment, but slow music with expressive timing (rubato) weakens it — yet slow music can increase *cardiac* synchrony via shared respiratory entrainment. Direction of causality (does synchrony cause pleasure, or does pleasure cause synchrony?) remains debated.

**Strongest Evidence**
- Asthagiri & Loui (2025): Live violin performance increased cerebro-acoustic phase-locking by ~31% over matched recordings; this predicted pleasure ratings (β = 2.85, p &lt; .001).
- Tschacher et al. (2023/2024): 695 concert-goers showed significant heart-rate and HRV synchrony; openness correlated with higher synchrony, anxiety with lower.
- Xygalatas et al. (2024): Hindu procession participants showed heightened autonomic synchrony compared to an unstructured walk along the same route, demonstrating that *ritual structure* — not just proximity — drives coupling.

## 2. Emotional Contagion Spread Across Groups

**Mechanism**  
Emotional contagion operates through *primitive emotional contagion* (Hatfield et al.): an automatic, unconscious process of mimicry → feedback → internalization. Facial expressions, vocal tone, and postural cues are mimicked via mirror neuron systems; this feeds back to the limbic system, generating congruent affective states. In groups, this scales through *circular reaction* (Blumer): one individual’s emotional display reproduces stimulation in others, which is reflected back and intensified. At the population level, this cascades via network amplifiers (shared identity + attention synchronization).

**Trigger**
- High-salience emotional cue from a focal individual or subgroup (e.g., a dancer, a charismatic patron, or a performer).
- Shared social identity making the group receptive to in-group normative emotions.
- Physical density that increases nonverbal cue exposure (within 1.5 m maximizes facial mimicry).

**Expected Behavioral Effect**
- Convergence of self-reported mood within 5–15 minutes in small groups; faster in high-arousal contexts.
- Increased cooperation or conflict depending on valence of the seeded emotion (positive contagion → cooperation; negative → conflict).
- Behavioral imitation: drinking pace, vocal volume, and postural expansion/contraction align with the emotionally expressive “sender.”

**Measurable Indicators**
- *Facial*: FACS-coded valence/arousal from video; micro-expression spread latency.
- *Vocal*: Fundamental frequency (F0) convergence; speech rate alignment.
- *Physiological*: Electrodermal activity (EDA) synchrony; HR convergence.
- *Behavioral*: Drink-sip timing correlation; posture mimicry indices.
- *Self-report*: Real-time mood sliders (e.g., 1–4 chills/pleasure scales).

**Possible KPI Candidates**
- Group mood convergence coefficient (standard deviation of real-time affect ratings).
- EDA synchrony ratio (high-sync periods / total observed periods).
- “Patient zero” identification: time for seeded emotion to reach 50% of zone.
- Behavioral mimicry index (cross-correlation of sip/head-nod times).

**Contextual Limitations**
- Contagion requires *attention gating*; if patrons are absorbed in dyadic conversation, they are less susceptible to ambient emotional cues.
- Alcohol intoxication disrupts facial mimicry accuracy but may increase emotional *expression* amplitude, creating asymmetric contagion.
- Digital media (phones) act as attentional shields, fragmenting contagion cascades.

**Contradictions / Uncertainty**
- New Social Movement theorists argue contagion models treat emotion as irrational; strategic emotion research shows emotions can be *tools* rather than purely contagious pathogens. The direction of influence (leader-to-follower vs. peer-to-peer vs. follower-to-leader) is understudied in hospitality.
- Negative emotions (anger, anxiety) may spread faster than positive ones, but the evidence is mixed across bar vs. concert contexts.

**Strongest Evidence**
- Barsade (2002): Causal test in managerial negotiation groups; a confederate’s positive/negative mood significantly shifted group mood and cooperative behavior, confirmed by video-coder ratings, peer ratings, and self-reports.
- Hatfield, Cacioppo & Rapson (1994): Found that emotionally expressive individuals “infected” others’ emotions without verbal interaction.
- Frontiers (2025) review: Mirror neuron systems and automatic mimicry facilitate rapid affective spread; cascades are multi-modal (emotional → perceptual → behavioral).

## 3. Collective Attention Emergence and Collapse

**Mechanism**  
Collective attention is a *joint attention* state scaled to groups. It emerges when individuals shift from *individual phase* (self-directed, fragmented attention) to *conforming phase* (group-directed, synchronized attention). Under the Social Identity Approach, this is enabled by three transformations: (1) cognitive — self-categorization as “audience member”; (2) relational — recognition of shared identity; (3) affective — empowerment to express group norms. Attention synchronizes via *attention gating*: when multiple individuals orient toward the same focal point, the probability of mimicry and physiological coupling increases. Collapse occurs when the focal point loses salience or competing stimuli fragment attention.

**Trigger**
- *Emergence*: Performer breaks fourth wall; sudden change in lighting/sound; drop in music volume creating anticipation; ritualized call-and-response.
- *Collapse*: Performer exits; house lights up; ambient noise floor rises above performance SPL; phone notifications; bar service interruption.

**Expected Behavioral Effect**
- *Emergence*: Orienting reflex (head turns, body rotation toward stage); reduction in conversational volume; pupillary dilation; increased HR.
- *Collapse*: Conversation restart; movement toward bar/restroom; phone checking; posture relaxation; desynchronization of previously aligned movements.

**Measurable Indicators**
- *Gaze*: Percentage of patrons facing focal point (computer vision).
- *Posture*: Forward lean vs. recline; shoulder orientation variance.
- *Acoustic*: Conversational SPL (dB) in audience zones; spectral flux toward speech bands.
- *Physiological*: HR deceleration (orienting response) then acceleration (engagement).
- *Neural*: Frontal theta increase (cognitive effort/engagement) followed by alpha drop.

**Possible KPI Candidates**
- Attention coherence index (% patrons oriented toward focal point / time).
- Conversation suppression ratio (SPL during performance vs. baseline).
- “Attention half-life”: time from collapse trigger to 50% conversation restart.
- Phone-screen illumination events per minute.

**Contextual Limitations**
- VIP/hospitality areas may sustain dyadic attention pockets even during performance peaks.
- Alcohol reduces orienting response amplitude but may prolong attention dwell time due to impaired task-switching.
- Visual obstructions (columns, height differences) create attention fragmentation regardless of performance quality.

**Contradictions / Uncertainty**
- Reicher’s ESIM suggests crowd attention is *normative* and rational within the group’s frame, whereas classical theories treat it as irrational hypnotic trance. In hospitality, both may apply: patrons may rationally attend to a performer while simultaneously being “captured” by rhythmic entrainment.
- The temporal dynamics of attention collapse are poorly quantified; most studies use post-hoc recall rather than continuous measurement.

**Strongest Evidence**
- Dikker et al. (2017): Brain-to-brain synchrony tracked real-world group interactions in classrooms; attention gating increased neural coupling.
- Goldsmiths NEUROLIVE (2025): Live contemporary dance audiences showed EEG synchrony indicating shared focus; synchrony dropped when same performance viewed alone on video.
- Tarnow’s two-phase model (cited in EBSCO): Crowds alternate between individual and conforming phases; behavioral norms shift accordingly.

## 4. Synchronized Engagement Manifested Physically and Behaviorally

**Mechanism**  
Synchronized engagement is the overt motor expression of underlying neural/physiological coupling. It includes *interpersonal entrainment* (unintentional coordination of body movement) and *collective effervescence* (Durkheim) — the ritualized build-up of shared arousal manifesting as clapping, dancing, or chanting. In hospitality, this appears as synchronized drinking gestures, head-nodding, or collective movement toward/away from performance zones. The mechanism is partly mediated by action-perception coupling: observing another’s rhythmic movement primes the motor cortex for matching action.

**Trigger**
- High-groove music (syncopated, medium-fast tempo with clear backbeat).
- Visible models (performers, enthusiastic patrons) providing rhythmic visual cues.
- Removal of physical barriers (tables, rails) that impede movement.
- Alcohol at moderate levels (reduces motor inhibition without impairing timing).

**Expected Behavioral Effect**
- Head-nodding, foot-tapping, or swaying in temporal alignment with music and neighbors.
- Increased drink consumption pace matching tempo.
- Spatial clustering near performance source; reduced variance in body orientation.
- Post-performance prosocial behavior (increased tipping, conversation initiation).

**Measurable Indicators**
- *Motion*: Accelerometer-derived movement energy; cross-correlation of head-nod times between neighbors.
- *Spatial*: Proxemic density maps; flow vectors toward stage.
- *Temporal*: Inter-sip interval distribution; alignment to beat phase.
- *Social*: Conversation initiation rate; physical touch frequency (high-fives, shoulder taps).

**Possible KPI Candidates**
- Synchronized movement index (% of patrons moving within ±50 ms of beat).
- Flow convergence entropy (lower entropy = higher directional alignment).
- Drink-to-beat phase-locking (for bars with rhythmic music).
- Post-set social interaction rate.

**Contextual Limitations**
- Seating vs. standing configurations radically alter movement expression; seated patrons show only head/upper-body synchrony.
- Cultural norms vary: some populations exhibit high-groove response through stillness and internal focus rather than overt movement.
- Temperature and attire (coats vs. light clothing) constrain movement range.

**Contradictions / Uncertainty**
- Swarbrick et al. found audiences move *more* to live than recorded music, but the relationship between movement quantity and movement *synchrony* is unclear — more movement may reflect individual arousal rather than collective coupling.
- Action-perception coupling is stronger in musicians; general audiences may rely more on vestibular/proprioceptive feedback than visual modeling.

**Strongest Evidence**
- Swarbrick et al. (2019): Head movement differences in audiences to live vs. recorded music; live conditions produced greater movement and synchrony.
- Grahn et al. (LIVELab, 2018): High-groove songs produced greater delta-band synchrony and head-movement coupling; network analysis showed “hub” synchronizers who connected multiple others.
- Chabin et al. (2022): Theta IBS during live music was highest when both group emotional convergence *and* group emotional strength were high; simple alignment without intensity did not increase synchrony.

## 5. Crowd Momentum vs. Crowd Fragmentation

**Mechanism**  
Momentum emerges when positive feedback loops amplify collective behavior: synchronized action → perceived unity → increased individual effort → greater synchrony. Under ESIM, this is *empowerment* — the affective transformation that makes group goals feel achievable. Fragmentation occurs when feedback loops invert: an external challenge (e.g., police action, service failure, or acoustic dropout) is perceived as illegitimate or indiscriminate, causing a shift from diverse individual identities to a unified *oppositional* identity. Paradoxically, this can also create momentum, but of a conflictual type. In hospitality, fragmentation more commonly arises from *normative divergence*: subgroups with different expectations (dancers vs. talkers) create friction that breaks the shared attention field.

**Trigger**
- *Momentum*: Successful call-and-response; drop in music (EDM “drop”); visible staff enthusiasm; rising temperature/SPL within comfort zone.
- *Fragmentation*: Acoustic dropout &gt;2 seconds; bar queue &gt;5 minutes; temperature &gt;26°C; arrival of a disruptive subgroup; performer mistake.

**Expected Behavioral Effect**
- *Momentum*: Collective cheering; increased drink orders; reduced phone use; spatial compression toward focal point; decreased variance in behavior.
- *Fragmentation*: Conversation clusters fragment; movement toward exits or bar; phone use spikes; increased variance in body orientation; territorial behavior (saving seats, blocking paths).

**Measurable Indicators**
- *Behavioral entropy*: Variance in movement direction, posture, and gaze across the crowd (low entropy = momentum; high entropy = fragmentation).
- *Phone-use density*: Screen-on events per m².
- *Order velocity*: Drinks ordered per minute at bar.
- *Exit flow*: Patrons leaving per minute relative to baseline.
- *Acoustic*: Crowd noise SPL and spectral content (cheering vs. speech).

**Possible KPI Candidates**
- Entropy index (0 = perfect momentum, 1 = random fragmentation).
- Momentum persistence (seconds/minutes a synchronized state sustains without external refresh).
- Fragmentation recovery time (time to re-synchronize after disruption).
- Subgroup isolation index (modularity of social network based on proximity/interaction).

**Contextual Limitations**
- Momentum is genre-dependent; jazz audiences may value fragmented, individual attention states as “sophisticated.”
- Fragmentation in high-end lounges may be *desired* (intimate dyadic conversation is the product).
- Staff intervention (clearing glasses, security presence) can either restore or fragment depending on perceived legitimacy.

**Contradictions / Uncertainty**
- ESIM predicts that indiscriminate external pressure unifies crowds, yet nightclub data show that crowding + heat + noise fragment behavior into territorial subgroups. The difference may be whether the stressor is *internal* (crowding) vs. *external* (police/staff).
- Tarnow’s model suggests crowds alternate phases naturally; it is unclear whether “momentum” is a stable attractor or a transient fluctuation.

**Strongest Evidence**
- Reicher, Drury & Stott (ESIM): Indiscriminate police action against protesters created shared fate and unified an otherwise diverse crowd, increasing conflictual momentum.
- Macintyre & Homel (1997): Nightclub crowding increased group territoriality (r = .424) and rowdiness (r = .568), which correlated with aggression (.447), showing fragmentation into hostile subgroups under density stress.
- St Pauls riot studies (Reicher, 1984/1987): Crowd action was patterned and normative, not irrational; behavior reflected shared social identity rather than mindless contagion.

## 6. Fatigue, Overstimulation, Density, and Environmental Pressure

**Mechanism**  
Overstimulation arises when sensory input (noise, light, motion, smell) exceeds cognitive processing capacity, triggering attentional narrowing and eventually cognitive fatigue. In hospitality, this is compounded by *density-stress*: the subjective experience of crowding (distinct from objective density) activates the HPA axis and sympathetic arousal. The *Density-Intensity Hypothesis* states that crowded conditions amplify both positive and negative experiences — excitement becomes euphoria, but discomfort becomes aggression. Heat, humidity, and tobacco smoke (where present) interact additively with density to increase alcohol consumption as a “cooling” behavior, which paradoxically increases intoxication and risk.

**Trigger**
- SPL sustained &gt;90 dB for &gt;30 minutes.
- Patron density &gt;0.5 persons/m² in movement zones (Macintyre & Homel’s high-risk threshold).
- Temperature &gt;24°C with poor ventilation.
- Multimodal overstimulation: strobe lights + bass vibration + speech masking.
- Time-on-site &gt;2.5 hours without sensory variation.

**Expected Behavioral Effect**
- *Early*: Increased drinking pace (self-medication for discomfort); reduced conversation complexity; increased physical contact/bumping.
- *Mid*: Irritability, territoriality, queue-jumping; reduced impulse control; emergence of “restlessness” (Blumer).
- *Late*: Social withdrawal (phone use, sitting); early exit; aggressive reactions to minor transgressions (spilled drinks, bumps).

**Measurable Indicators**
- *Physiological*: HR elevation; HRV reduction (RMSSD drop); EDA elevation indicating sympathetic dominance.
- *Behavioral*: Drink-sip frequency increase; restroom visit rate; phone-checking frequency; exit rate.
- *Environmental*: SPL, temperature, CO₂, and PM2.5 (smoke/vape) readings.
- *Spatial*: Movement path tortuosity (fatigued patrons take less direct routes).

**Possible KPI Candidates**
- Overstimulation index (weighted composite of SPL, temp, density, time).
- Fatigue threshold (% patrons showing HRV suppression + phone withdrawal).
- Aggression probability score (density × temperature × male-density × time).
- Cooling-drink velocity (alcoholic vs. non-alcoholic shift).

**Contextual Limitations**
- Density-intensity is culturally moderated: some Asian night markets thrive at densities that would fragment Western clubs.
- “Fatigue” in a festival context may be accepted as part of the ritual; in a lounge it signals product failure.
- Female presence moderates male crowding stress (Dabbs, 1977); gender mix is a critical covariate.

**Contradictions / Uncertainty**
- Baron & Bell (1975) found heat facilitates aggression in calm subjects but can reduce it in already-aroused subjects (negative affect escape model). The interaction with alcohol is nonlinear and poorly modeled.
- Macintyre & Homel note that better-designed venues may attract better-managed crowds, confounding design effects with selection effects.

**Strongest Evidence**
- Macintyre & Homel (1997): 36 visits to 6 nightclubs found crowding correlated with aggression at r = .88; relationship was nonlinear and stronger in high-risk venues. Design features (door/bar/dancefloor placement) mediated density-to-crowding conversion.
- Griffit & Veitch (1971): Overcrowded + hot conditions caused deterioration of social behavior parallel to animal studies.
- Psychology Town (2025): Chronic crowding exposure triggers fight-or-flight (HR, BP, shallow breathing) and long-term stress/anxiety.

## 7. Music Interaction with Collective Behavioral States

**Mechanism**  
Music acts as a *scaffold* for collective behavior through three pathways: (1) *arousal modulation* — tempo and volume directly alter autonomic tone; (2) *entrainment* — beat structure provides a shared temporal reference for motor synchronization; (3) *affective priming* — mode (major/minor) and timbre shift group emotion valence. In hospitality, tempo is the most robustly causal variable: fast music (&gt;120 BPM) increases arousal, reduces cognitive control, and accelerates consumption behavior; slow music (80–100 BPM) extends dwell time and increases per-capita spend on alcohol. Volume operates independently: loud music (&gt;85 dB) increases alcohol consumption and speed by reducing conversation opportunities and increasing arousal.

**Trigger**
- Tempo shift: DJ transitions from 100 BPM to 128 BPM.
- Volume shift: +6 dB SPL increase during chorus/drop.
- Genre/mode shift: minor key → major key transition.
- Live vs. recorded transition (live produces stronger neural and physiological coupling).

**Expected Behavioral Effect**
- *Fast/high-volume*: Faster drinking, shorter inter-purchase intervals, increased movement/dancing, earlier exit unless peak experience sustains.
- *Slow/low-volume*: Longer dwell, higher check size, more conversation, increased likelihood of food order.
- *Live performance*: Greater head movement, increased perceived social bonding, higher return-visit intent.

**Measurable Indicators**
- *Acoustic*: BPM, SPL, spectral centroid, beat salience.
- *Behavioral*: Inter-sip interval, bar queue length, dancefloor occupancy, conversation SPL.
- *Physiological*: HR acceleration; EDA rise during tempo shifts.
- *Economic*: Revenue per patron per hour; drink category mix (shots vs. cocktails vs. water).

**Possible KPI Candidates**
- Tempo-consumption elasticity (Δ drinks / Δ BPM).
- Live-music premium (revenue differential live vs. recorded, controlling for genre).
- Dwell-time elasticity per 10 BPM decrease.
- Beat-phase movement coupling index.

**Contextual Limitations**
- Congruity matters: fast music during a relaxed brunch creates negative affect; slow music during a pre-game sports bar reduces satisfaction.
- Musical training moderates entrainment strength; general audiences may respond more to volume and lyrics than to rhythmic complexity.
- Repeated exposure to the same tempo range causes habituation; novelty is required to sustain arousal effects.

**Contradictions / Uncertainty**
- Some studies find tempo has no effect on *perceived* duration (Bailey, 2006) or actual dwell time in bars (one field study found no significant difference), yet most find effects on spending. The discrepancy may be due to genre congruity or time-of-day moderation.
- Stroebele & Castro (2006) found music *presence* increased intake but tempo/volume had no effect, contradicting Guéguen et al. (2008) and McElrea & Standing (1992).

**Strongest Evidence**
- McElrea & Standing (1992): Fast music significantly decreased drinking time in experimental bar conditions.
- Guéguen et al. (2008): Loud music increased alcohol consumption and drinking speed, partly via reduced social interaction.
- Asthagiri & Loui (2025): Live performance increased neural phase-locking by 31% over matched recordings; this predicted pleasure/engagement.
- Caldwell & Hibbert (2002): Slow tempo increased time spent and spending in a bar; normal tempo fell between slow and fast for income generation.

## 8. Crowd Energy Propagation Through Groups

**Mechanism**  
“Crowd energy” is an emergent property of synchronized arousal propagating through a network. It spreads via *perceptual contagion* (observing others’ excited states primes matching affect), *motor resonance* (mirror neuron activation by vigorous movement), and *acoustic coupling* (collective cheering increases SPL, which further elevates arousal). In physical crowds, this follows a *wave* model: energy originates at a focal point (performer, enthusiastic subgroup) and propagates at a velocity determined by visual line-of-sight and physical proximity. In dense crowds, propagation is faster but may saturate or fragment into local “hot spots” rather than uniform fields.

**Trigger**
- Performer-perceived enthusiasm (smiling, movement, eye contact).
- “Super-spreader” patrons: highly expressive individuals with high network centrality.
- Acoustic feedback: crowd noise → SPL increase → collective arousal.
- Ritualized release points: chorus drops, countdowns, call-and-response.

**Expected Behavioral Effect**
- Wave of standing/sitting transitions moving through audience.
- Cascading applause/cheering with temporal structure (not random).
- Increased movement amplitude as energy propagates.
- Bar-order spikes following energy peaks (patrons “reward” themselves after collective highs).

**Measurable Indicators**
- *Network*: Synchrony network centrality (who synchronizes with most others).
- *Temporal*: Cross-correlation lag between movement onset in front rows vs. back rows.
- *Acoustic*: Cheering onset detection; SPL rise time.
- *Economic*: POS transaction timestamps aligned to performance peaks.
- *Physiological*: EDA surge propagation across wearable arrays.

**Possible KPI Candidates**
- Energy propagation velocity (meters/second or rows/second).
- Amplification ratio (final crowd arousal / initial focal-point arousal).
- Super-spreader identification (nodes with highest out-degree in synchrony network).
- Revenue lag from energy peak (seconds between cheer and bar order).

**Contextual Limitations**
- Propagation requires visual connectivity; obstructed sightlines create “dead zones.”
- Alcohol nonlinearly affects propagation: moderate doses increase expressivity; high doses impair motor timing and reduce synchrony.
- Energy can propagate negative valence (booing, sit-down protests) through identical network mechanisms.

**Contradictions / Uncertainty**
- Chabin et al. found that simple emotional alignment (many people feeling the same thing) did *not* increase theta IBS unless the emotion was also *intense*. This suggests energy propagation requires intensity, not just consensus.
- Whether energy propagation is better modeled as contagion (individual-to-individual) or as field effects (uniform environmental influence) is unresolved.

**Strongest Evidence**
- Grahn LIVELab (2018): Network analysis showed some audience members acted as “hubs” with higher synchrony to multiple others; hub degree predicted enjoyment and performer connection.
- Chabin et al. (2022): Theta IBS was highest when both group emotional convergence *and* group emotional strength were high; low-intensity alignment produced no synchrony.
- Frontiers (2025): Cascading-resonance model shows contagion types scaffold each other (emotional → perceptual → behavioral), forming multi-modal cascades.

## 9. Measurable Real-World Signals

### Synchronization
- **Neural**: EEG delta/theta PLV; cerebro-acoustic phase-locking.
- **Physiological**: HR inter-subject correlation; HRV time-frequency coherence; EDA synchrony.
- **Motor**: Head-nod/tap onset cross-correlation; movement energy covariance.
- **Spatial**: Body orientation variance; proxemic alignment.

### Emotional Alignment
- **Facial**: FACS valence/arousal convergence; micro-expression spread latency.
- **Vocal**: F0 and speech-rate convergence.
- **Physiological**: EDA tonic level covariance; HR acceleration/deceleration alignment.
- **Self-report**: Real-time affect slider variance across group (low variance = high alignment).

### Engagement
- **Attention**: Gaze fixation duration on focal point; conversation SPL suppression.
- **Movement**: Dancefloor occupancy; head-nod amplitude; phone-screen-on rate.
- **Economic**: Drink order velocity; dwell time; app engagement (polls, Q&A).
- **Neural**: Frontal theta increase; alpha suppression.

### Fatigue
- **Physiological**: HRV RMSSD drop; HR elevation without movement; pupillary constriction.
- **Behavioral**: Phone-use increase; restroom visits; posture slump; reduced conversation initiation.
- **Spatial**: Path tortuosity; reduced movement range.
- **Temporal**: Time-on-site &gt;2.5 hours with monotonic environment.

### Overstimulation
- **Environmental**: SPL &gt;90 dB; temperature &gt;24°C; CO₂ &gt;1000 ppm; lighting flicker/strobe frequency.
- **Physiological**: EDA sustained elevation; HR max without rhythmic structure; blink rate suppression.
- **Behavioral**: Rapid task-switching; irritability cues (aggressive gesturing); territorial blocking; early exit.

### Momentum Collapse
- **Entropy**: Movement direction variance spike; gaze dispersion.
- **Acoustic**: Crowd noise shift from rhythmic cheering to diffuse speech.
- **Economic**: Order velocity drop; exit flow increase.
- **Network**: Synchrony network fragmentation (modularity increase).

## System Design Implications for Polynovea

For a live behavioral experimentation system in hospitality venues, the evidence supports a **multi-modal, zoned measurement architecture**:

1. **Neural/Physiological Layer**: Wearable or non-contact EEG/ECG arrays in performance zones to track delta-band PLV and HR synchrony as real-time “bonding” indices.
2. **Acoustic Layer**: Continuous BPM and SPL monitoring with automated alerts when combinations exceed fatigue thresholds (e.g., &gt;120 BPM + &gt;90 dB + &gt;0.5 persons/m² + &gt;25°C).
3. **Visual Layer**: Computer-vision tracking of gaze orientation, movement entropy, and phone-use density to detect attention collapse before it manifests in exits.
4. **Economic Layer**: POS timestamp alignment to performance and environmental metrics to measure revenue elasticity to crowd states.
5. **Environmental Layer**: Temperature, CO₂, and humidity sensors to model density-heat-stress interactions.

The strongest causal levers available to operators are **tempo**, **SPL**, **density management**, and **live performance scheduling** — each with robust experimental evidence for direct behavioral effects. The deepest uncertainty lies in the temporal dynamics of state transitions: exactly how fast does a crowd shift from “individual phase” to “conforming phase,” and what is the minimum “dose” of rhythmic entrainment required to prevent fragmentation? These are the questions a live experimentation system should prioritize.
