## 1. The Peak-End Rule in Experiential Contexts

### 1.1 Original Research & Foundational Evidence

The Peak-End Rule was first formalized by **Barbara Fredrickson and Daniel Kahneman** in a 1993 paper titled *"When More Pain Is Preferred to Less: Adding a Better End."* The central claim is that people's retrospective evaluation of a hedonic episode â€” any extended experience of pleasure or pain â€” is almost entirely predicted by two data points: the **peak emotional intensity** reached during the episode, and the **emotional intensity in the final moments**. The rest of the experience is averaged out of memory.îŽ citeîŽŁweb_search:1#1îŽŁweb_search:1#8îŽ¨

The classic **cold-water experiment** established the effect empirically. Participants held their hand in 14Â°C water for 60 seconds in one trial, and in another trial held their hand in 14Â°C water for 60 seconds followed by an additional 30 seconds as the water warmed to 15Â°C â€” still painful, but milder. When asked which trial they would prefer to repeat, a majority chose the *longer* trial. They preferred a strictly greater total amount of pain because the remembering self read the gentler ending as "this wasn't so bad." The experiencing self suffered more; the remembering self judged the longer version as less aversive; the remembering self's judgment predicted future choice.îŽ citeîŽŁweb_search:1#6îŽŁweb_search:1#8îŽ¨

Kahneman and Redelmeier extended this to **colonoscopy patients** in a 1996 randomized trial. Some patients experienced a typical colonoscopy; others underwent a modified, slightly longer procedure that ended less painfully. The modified procedure was associated with a significantly lower memory of pain, a less painful memory of the procedure overall, and â€” critically â€” an **increased likelihood of returning for another colonoscopy** years later. Patients were aware of the event's length but did not incorporate this into their judgments.îŽ citeîŽŁweb_search:1#10îŽŁweb_search:1#4îŽ¨

A **2025 meta-analysis** (Alaybek et al., 2025) synthesized the literature and found: the **peak effect was large**, the **end effect was medium-sized**, and the **peak-end average effect was large**. Duration neglect was confirmed as a robust phenomenon across studies.îŽ citeîŽŁweb_search:1#3îŽ¨

### 1.2 The Experiencing Self vs. The Remembering Self

Kahneman's framework distinguishes two selves:
- **The experiencing self**: lives each moment, integrates pleasure/pain moment-by-moment (the "area under the curve")
- **The remembering self**: constructs a compressed summary afterwards, using only peak and end intensity to decide whether to repeat the experience

After a moment has passed, only the remembering self exists when thinking about our past lives. **The remembering self is the one making future decisions.** This leads to preferences that the experiencing self would find odd â€” and shows that we cannot trust our preferences to reflect our interests.îŽ citeîŽŁweb_search:1#6îŽŁweb_search:1#8îŽ¨

> "We don't choose between experiences, we choose between memories of experiences." â€” Daniel Kahneman

### 1.3 Duration Neglect: Why a 4-Hour Night and a 2-Hour Night May Be Remembered Equally

**Duration neglect** is the psychological principle that the length of an experience has little effect on the memory of that event. In the cold-water study, doubling the length of a painful experience made it remembered as *less* aversive if the added time had a gentler intensity. For pleasant experiences, the same logic applies: a longer pleasant experience is not necessarily remembered as more valuable than a shorter one of equivalent peak and end.îŽ citeîŽŁweb_search:1#6îŽŁweb_search:1#12îŽ¨

The practical implication for nightlife: **the shape of the intensity curve matters more than the area under it.** A 2-hour night with a powerful peak and a graceful end will be remembered more fondly than a 4-hour night with a flat emotional profile and a poor ending. The 4-hour night may deliver more total pleasure to the experiencing self, but the remembering self compresses it to two snapshots.

This is not to say duration is irrelevant â€” for very long pleasant episodes (e.g., multi-day vacations), some duration effect does accumulate. But for single-session nightlife experiences (2â€“6 hours), duration neglect is the dominant pattern.îŽ citeîŽŁweb_search:1#0îŽ¨

### 1.4 The Neuroscience of Peak-End Memory Encoding

Three converging neuroscience strands explain why the Peak-End Rule exists:

**1. Hippocampal boundary encoding:** The hippocampus slices continuous experience into discrete events with identifiable boundaries. Encoding spikes at those boundaries. The "peak" is a high-affect boundary; the "end" is a structural boundary. Memory encoding mechanisms privilege both.îŽ citeîŽŁweb_search:1#0îŽ¨

**2. Amygdala salience tagging:** The amygdala tags emotionally salient moments for preferential storage. Cahill and McGaugh's research shows that high-arousal moments â€” whether positive or negative â€” produce enhanced long-term retention compared to neutral moments of identical duration. The peak earns more storage resources at encoding and preferentially survives retrieval.îŽ citeîŽŁweb_search:3#0îŽŁweb_search:3#3îŽ¨

**3. Recency effect:** The final moments of an experience are disproportionately re-activated during retrieval â€” the serial-position "recency effect" is one of the oldest findings in cognitive psychology (Ebbinghaus 1885; Murdock 1962). The ending is not just encoded well; it is also retrieved first when we try to remember the experience, anchoring the global evaluation.îŽ citeîŽŁweb_search:1#0îŽŁweb_search:2#4îŽ¨

### 1.5 Boundary Conditions: When the Peak-End Rule Strengthens or Weakens

The 2025 meta-analysis and subsequent research identified important moderators:

- **For simple, short, aversive experiences** (colonoscopies, cold water), the rule is strongest.
- **For complex, heterogeneous, multi-episodic experiences** (theme park vacations, full nights out), the rule still holds but interacts with narrative coherence and contextual meaning. A 2021 study on musical theatre in a theme park found that peak-end theory was *rejected* for the experience as a whole, but *supported* for individual episodes within the experience.îŽ citeîŽŁweb_search:2#11îŽ¨
- **For positive experiences** (concerts, holidays), the rule applies but duration neglect is slightly softer â€” a longer positive experience can accumulate some remembered value above a shorter one of equivalent peak and end.îŽ citeîŽŁweb_search:1#0îŽ¨
- **Expectations matter:** If expectations are not met at the start, the mind evaluates everything through a critical lens, discounting peaks and amplifying negative endings.îŽ citeîŽŁweb_search:1#7îŽ¨

**Implication for nightlife:** A night is a multi-episodic experience (arrival, warm-up, peak, wind-down, departure). The Peak-End Rule operates at *both* the episode level and the whole-night level. Each episode has its own peak and end; the night as a whole has its meta-peak and meta-end.

## 2. Engineering the Peak

### 2.1 What Constitutes a Genuine Emotional Peak in Nightlife

In the Heath brothers' framework (*The Power of Moments*, 2017), peak moments cluster around four families: **Elevation, Insight, Pride, and Connection**. The most memorable experiences engineer peak moments within these families.îŽ citeîŽŁweb_search:1#0îŽŁweb_search:4#5îŽ¨

For nightlife specifically, a genuine emotional peak is a moment where:
- **Elevation**: Sensory pleasure exceeds baseline â€” music, lighting, and atmosphere converge to create a "break from the script"
- **Connection**: Collective synchrony produces perceived emotional unison (Durkheim's *collective effervescence*)
- **Arousal**: Emotional intensity reaches a local maximum, triggering amygdala-mediated memory consolidation

**Collective effervescence** â€” Ă‰mile Durkheim's concept â€” describes the emotional energy that emerges when people come together around a shared experience. In a mass event, people experience multifaceted synchronization: shared time, place, attentional focus, actions, expressions, and emotional responses. The combined effects stimulate participants' experience of similar emotional states, fueling fusion or unison. PĂˇez et al. (2015) operationalized this as **Perceived Emotional Synchrony (PES)**, measured by items like "We felt a strong shared emotion" and "We performed as one, like a single person."îŽ citeîŽŁweb_search:2#8îŽ¨

### 2.2 The Neuroscience of Crowd Synchrony

Recent neuroscience research on live performance provides empirical evidence for collective peaks:

**UCL NEUROLIVE Study (2025):** Researchers outfitted 59 audience members with EEG headsets during three live contemporary dance performances. They found that audience members' **brainwaves synchronized in the delta band** during live shows â€” a range of slow-frequency brainwaves associated with mind-wandering and social processing. This synchrony was *especially strong when performers made direct eye contact with the crowd*. When the same performance was watched alone on video, synchrony weakened. The researchers concluded that "social liveness" may be as important as the performance itself.îŽ citeîŽŁweb_search:3#2îŽŁweb_search:3#6îŽ¨

**Biometric Concert Study (NeuroLynQ):** A live classical music concert measured galvanic skin response (GSR) from 30 audience members. The most striking finding: **the peaks between songs** â€” clapping and cheering â€” were the most arousing part of the concert. Listening to the music was comparatively relaxing; GSR levels declined through every song. The group effect (shared applause) reinforced the positive audience response. The performers' and audience's responses were remarkably similar.îŽ citeîŽŁweb_search:3#5îŽ¨

**The Synchronicity Hypothesis of Dance** proposes that humans dance to enhance both intra- and inter-brain synchrony. When exposed to similar emotional experiences (e.g., emotional movies), neural synchrony between individuals increases in sensory cortical areas and limbic regions (amygdala, insula, thalamus), with the **intensity of the emotional experience correlated to the level of neural synchrony**.îŽ citeîŽŁweb_search:3#4îŽ¨

**Implication:** The peak in a nightlife context is not just a musical climax â€” it is a **neurobiological synchrony event** where the crowd's brains temporarily align. This is why the peak feels "bigger than yourself" and why it is remembered with such intensity.

### 2.3 How to Create a Peak Moment Intentionally

The convergence of three elements produces the most powerful peaks:

**1. Music:** The drop, the key change, the tempo shift â€” these are structural peaks in the auditory experience. Rozin et al. (2004) found that steeper slopes in moment-to-moment emotional intensity led to higher overall evaluations, suggesting that the *build-up* to a peak is as important as the peak itself.îŽ citeîŽŁweb_search:2#0îŽ¨

**2. Lighting:** Strobe and effect lights are most effective when triggered during key moments (drops, transitions, climactic sections). When used sparingly, they can instantly raise energy levels and refresh the visual atmosphere. Haze/atmosphere adds depth, making beams and movement more visible and immersive.îŽ citeîŽŁweb_search:2#1îŽ¨

**3. Crowd Synchrony:** The peak is amplified when the crowd moves, sings, or responds as one. The NeuroLynQ study showed that clapping/cheering between songs produced higher arousal than the music itself because everyone's response was amplified by the responses of those around them.îŽ citeîŽŁweb_search:3#5îŽ¨

**Design principle:** The peak should be a **multi-sensory convergence point** where auditory, visual, and social stimuli align. This is not additive â€” it is multiplicative. The music alone produces X arousal; the lighting alone produces Y; the crowd synchrony produces Z. Together, they produce something closer to X Ă— Y Ă— Z due to neural synchrony effects.

### 2.4 Organic vs. Engineered Peaks: Catalysis Over Force

The most powerful peaks in nightlife are **organic** â€” they emerge from the interaction between performer, crowd, and environment rather than being rigidly scripted. The UCL study found that choreographers could predict which moments would produce peak brain synchrony, but the synchrony itself was an emergent property of the live social context.îŽ citeîŽŁweb_search:3#2îŽ¨

**Why organic peaks are more powerful:**
- **Prediction error:** The brain is tuned to detect unexpected rewards. A perfectly predictable peak is less memorable than one that contains an element of surprise. The "oddball effect" shows that surprise stretches subjective time and enhances memory.îŽ citeîŽŁweb_search:4#7îŽ¨
- **Agency:** When the crowd feels they *created* the peak through their own energy, the emotional investment is higher. Perceived Emotional Synchrony is strongest when participants feel they are co-creating the experience.îŽ citeîŽŁweb_search:2#8îŽ¨
- **Narrative fit:** A peak that emerges naturally from the arc of the night feels "earned." A forced peak at an arbitrary time feels artificial and can produce cynicism.

**How to catalyze rather than force:**
- **Read the room:** Use real-time crowd energy signals (movement density, vocalization, phone-light density) to identify when the crowd is *ready* for a peak
- **Create conditions, not scripts:** Set up the musical, lighting, and atmospheric conditions that make a peak *likely*, but allow the exact timing to be responsive
- **Build slopes, not cliffs:** Rozin et al.'s "slope effect" shows that large positive differences between consecutive moments predict higher overall evaluation. A peak needs a ramp.îŽ citeîŽŁweb_search:2#0îŽ¨

### 2.5 Multiple Peaks vs. Single Peak: Which Produces Better Memory?

This is one of the most contested questions in the literature. The 2014 Frontiers in Psychology study on musical experience directly tested this:

**Key findings:**
- **Average of all moments** was the best predictor of overall evaluation (Î˛ = 0.59, p < 0.001)
- **Peak-end** was a significant second predictor (Î˛ = 0.26, p < 0.001)
- **Multiple peaks-end** performed similarly to single peak-end
- **Number of multiple peaks** had *no* significant influence on global rating (Î˛ = 0.01, p = 0.61)îŽ citeîŽŁweb_search:2#0îŽ¨

**Interpretation:** For the overall impression of a musical experience, it is essential that there is an outstanding peak, but it appears to make **no difference if there is only one peak or a series of multiple peaks**. What matters is that *at least one* peak reaches a threshold of emotional intensity, not that multiple peaks are distributed throughout.

However, this finding must be qualified:
- The study used *individual music listening*, not live social experiences. In a social context, multiple peaks may serve different functions (group bonding, energy maintenance, narrative progression).
- The "average of all moments" finding suggests that long passages of weak emotional intensity *do* lower the moving average and thus the retrospective evaluation. Multiple smaller peaks may prevent the average from dropping too low between major peaks.

**Practical recommendation for nightlife:**
- **One dominant peak** is sufficient for memory, but the *average* emotional intensity across the night also matters
- **Secondary peaks** should function as "average maintainers" â€” they prevent the emotional baseline from dropping so low that the dominant peak cannot compensate
- **Do not dilute the dominant peak:** A night with too many "peaks" ceases to have any true peak. The dominant peak must stand out clearly from the secondary ones

## 3. Engineering the End

### 3.1 The Closing Sequence: Structuring the Last 20 Minutes

The final 20 minutes of a night are disproportionately weighted in memory due to the **recency effect** â€” the last parts of an experience are still fresh in working memory when the experience concludes, making them more accessible for recall.îŽ citeîŽŁweb_search:2#4îŽ¨

The closing sequence should be structured as a **controlled descent**, not an abrupt stop:

**Minutes -20 to -10: The Transition**
- Begin reducing tempo/BPM gradually (not suddenly)
- Shift lighting from high-energy to warm/atmospheric
- Introduce recognizable or emotionally resonant music (not brand-new tracks)
- This signals to the crowd that the peak has passed and creates a "bridge" to the end

**Minutes -10 to -5: The Emotional Landing**
- The final musical selection should be chosen for emotional resonance, not energy
- This is the moment that will be averaged with the peak to form the global memory
- Lighting should be gentle, not dark â€” a "warm glow" rather than abrupt house lights

**Minutes -5 to 0: The Farewell**
- The actual last moments â€” the walk to the exit, the final interaction, the ambient sound as people leave
- This is the "end" in the Kahneman framework â€” the final emotional intensity reading

### 3.2 Emotional Landing: High vs. Gentle Resolution

Research on **well-rounded endings** (SchwĂ¶rer, Krott, & Oettingen, 2020) found that endings marked by a sense of closure â€” feeling that one has done everything possible, completed something to the fullest, tied up loose ends â€” are associated with:
- High positive affect
- Low regret
- Easy transition into the subsequent phaseîŽ citeîŽŁweb_search:4#8îŽ¨

In nightlife, this translates to two competing models:

**Model A: End on a High (The "Fireworks" Model)**
- The final moments are as intense as possible
- Produces a strong positive end-reading
- Risk: can feel abrupt; may create a "crash" feeling afterward
- Best for: one-time events, festivals, occasions where "epic" is the desired memory

**Model B: Gentle Resolution (The "Warm Glow" Model)**
- The final moments are pleasant but not intense
- Produces a sense of closure and completion
- Risk: if too flat, the end may not be memorable enough to positively weight the average
- Best for: recurring venues, building long-term loyalty, occasions where "I want to come back" is the desired outcome

**The colonoscopy research suggests Model B for return intent:** The patients who returned were those whose procedures ended *less painfully*, not *more dramatically*. A gentler end created a better memory and a higher willingness to repeat.îŽ citeîŽŁweb_search:1#10îŽ¨

However, the **Power of Moments** research suggests that peaks are what people *talk about*, while endings are what people *return for*. The optimal strategy may be:
- **Peak:** Maximum intensity (what they tell others about)
- **End:** Warm, gentle resolution (what makes them want to return)

### 3.3 The Farewell Moment: Lighting and Music at Last Call

The "farewell moment" is the final sensory input before the experience is sealed in memory. Research on **false memories for endings** shows that when events are interrupted before their expected endpoint, participants often falsely recall additional details beyond the actual endpoint â€” they "extend" the event in memory to fit expected narrative structure. Conversely, when events end at a coherent boundary, memory is more accurate and more positive.îŽ citeîŽŁweb_search:2#7îŽ¨

**Implications for last call and departure:**
- **Do not abruptly cut the music** â€” this creates an "incomplete" ending that the brain will try to resolve, often negatively
- **Do not let the space go silent** â€” silence is a negative end-reading
- **Use a "closing anthem"** â€” a single, emotionally resonant track that provides narrative closure
- **Lighting should signal "ending" clearly but warmly** â€” a gradual brightening (not sudden house lights) that allows the eye to adjust and the brain to register "this is the end"
- **The exit experience matters** â€” the 2â€“3 minutes between the music stopping and the person leaving the venue are part of the "end"

### 3.4 How a Poor Closing Sequence Can Undo a Great Night

A negative end is a **negative peak** â€” and because it is also the end, it is doubly weighted. The negativity bias (Baumeister et al., 2001) shows that negative events impact self-esteem and memory more than positive events of equal intensity. A poor ending can:

1. **Overwrite the peak:** If the end is sufficiently negative, it can dominate the peak-end average, making the entire night feel worse than it was
2. **Block return intent:** The colonoscopy study showed that a worse ending directly reduced willingness to return for repeat procedures. The same mechanism operates in nightlife.îŽ citeîŽŁweb_search:1#10îŽ¨
3. **Trigger negative word-of-mouth:** People share negative experiences more readily than positive ones. A bad ending is the story they will tell.îŽ citeîŽŁweb_search:4#2îŽ¨

**Common closing failures in nightlife:**
- Abrupt music cut + bright house lights = negative end-reading
- Long queues for coat check/exit = negative end-reading
- Aggressive security/staff behavior at closing = negative peak + negative end (doubly weighted)
- No "goodbye" ritual â€” the night just "fades out" = weak end-reading that fails to positively weight the average

## 4. Application to Show Engineering Decisions

### 4.1 How to Decide When the Peak Has Occurred and What to Do Next

The peak is not a fixed timestamp â€” it is an **emergent event** that can be detected through multiple signals:

**Real-time detection signals:**
- **Biometric proxy:** Crowd movement density, vocalization level, phone-light density (people raise phones at peaks)
- **Neural proxy:** In future deployments, wearable GSR or even EEG could measure arousal synchrony in real time
- **Temporal proxy:** In a well-designed set, the peak typically occurs 60â€“75% of the way through the main performance arc (not at the very end â€” the end is reserved for landing)

**Once the peak is detected or predicted:**
1. **Do not try to top it.** A second peak that attempts to exceed the first will likely fail and diminish the memory of the first. The "slope effect" suggests that the *decline* from an attempted second peak can produce a negative slope that lowers overall evaluation.îŽ citeîŽŁweb_search:2#0îŽ¨
2. **Begin the descent immediately.** The post-peak period should be a controlled reduction in intensity, not a plateau. A flat period after a peak is remembered as "boring" relative to the peak.
3. **Use the post-peak period for connection, not intensity.** This is where social bonding moments (singalongs, shared recognition, call-and-response) can maintain engagement without competing with the peak.

### 4.2 How to Time the Closing Sequence Based on What Happened in the Session

The closing sequence should be **responsive**, not scripted. Three scenarios:

**Scenario A: The Peak Was Exceptional (High Energy, Strong Synchrony)**
- The crowd is emotionally "spent" in a positive way
- Closing should be *gentle* â€” they don't need more intensity, they need closure
- Risk: an energetic closing will feel like "too much"
- Strategy: Warm, melodic, emotionally resonant final tracks; gradual lighting shift; allow the crowd to "come down" together

**Scenario B: The Peak Was Good But Not Exceptional**
- The crowd still has energy "in the tank"
- Closing can be *moderately* energetic â€” a "mini-peak" at the end that compensates for a mid-level main peak
- Strategy: A strong, recognizable final track that provides a satisfying conclusion without trying to recreate the main peak

**Scenario C: The Peak Never Really Happened (Flat Night, External Disruption)**
- The crowd is unsatisfied; the "peak" reading is low
- The end becomes *critical* â€” it is the only chance to salvage the memory
- Strategy: Create an artificial "peak" in the final 10 minutes through a surprise moment (unexpected track, special lighting, performer acknowledgment) followed immediately by a warm end. The surprise provides the peak; the warmth provides the end. This is the "service recovery" principle applied to show engineering.îŽ citeîŽŁweb_search:4#9îŽ¨

### 4.3 Post-Session Debrief: What to Log for Memory Arc Reconstruction

The goal of the debrief is to reconstruct the **memory arc** â€” the peak-end average that will determine whether guests return and what they tell others. The experiencing self's data (full moment-by-moment) is less important than the remembering self's data (peak and end snapshots).

**Log these variables immediately post-session:**

| Variable | What to Record | Why It Matters |
|----------|---------------|--------------|
| **Peak Timestamp** | When the highest crowd energy occurred | Determines the "peak" in the peak-end average |
| **Peak Intensity (1â€“10)** | Subjective rating by show engineer + objective proxy (noise level, movement) | Determines the weight of the peak in memory |
| **Peak Type** | Musical (drop/key change), Social (crowd singalong), Visual (lighting moment), Multi-sensory | Informs which levers to pull in future sessions |
| **Peak Organic vs. Engineered** | Did the peak emerge naturally or was it triggered by a specific intervention? | Organic peaks may be more memorable; engineered peaks may be more reliable |
| **End Timestamp** | When the music stopped / when the majority began leaving | Defines the "end" boundary |
| **End Intensity (1â€“10)** | Emotional tone of the final 10 minutes | Determines the "end" in the peak-end average |
| **End Type** | High energy, Gentle resolution, Abrupt, Chaotic | Predicts return intent vs. word-of-mouth |
| **Duration** | Total session length | For duration neglect analysis â€” does length correlate with memory? |
| **Pre-Peak Slope** | How quickly intensity built before the peak | The "slope effect" predicts overall evaluation |
| **Post-Peak Slope** | How quickly intensity declined after the peak | A steep drop is better than a flat plateau |
| **Crowd Synchrony Signal** | Was there a moment of collective behavior? (singalong, unified movement, applause) | Neural synchrony correlates with memory intensity |
| **External Disruption** | Any negative event (technical failure, security incident, weather) | Negative peaks can dominate if not managed |
| **Return Intent Proxy** | Social media mentions, repeat guest identification, ticket pre-sales for next event | The ultimate dependent variable |
| **Word-of-Mouth Signal** | What guests are saying in real-time (shoutouts, posts, comments) | Reveals which moments are being encoded for transmission |

**Debrief protocol:**
1. **Immediate (within 30 min):** Engineer logs peak/end timestamps and subjective ratings while memory is fresh
2. **Same-night:** Review any biometric data (GSR, movement sensors, noise levels) to correlate with subjective ratings
3. **Next-day:** Review social media posts and mentions to identify which moments guests are already talking about
4. **One-week:** Conduct short follow-up with a sample of guests (3â€“5 questions) to test the peak-end hypothesis â€” ask them to describe "the moment that stood out" and "how it ended"
5. **Pattern analysis:** After 10+ sessions, analyze which peak types, end types, and slope profiles correlate with highest return intent and word-of-mouth

### 4.4 The Memory Arc as a Decision Framework

The show engineering system should treat each session as an experiment in **memory construction**. The independent variables are:
- Peak intensity, timing, and type
- End intensity, timing, and type
- Slope profiles (pre-peak and post-peak)
- Crowd synchrony conditions

The dependent variables are:
- Return intent (measured by repeat attendance, pre-sales, loyalty sign-ups)
- Word-of-mouth (measured by social mentions, referral codes, organic posts)
- Overall satisfaction (measured by post-session micro-surveys focused on peak and end, not average)

**The operational mantra:** Baseline â†’ Profile â†’ Strategy â†’ Execute â†’ Measure â†’ Pattern â†’ Decision â†’ Scale. The Peak-End Rule provides the **measurement framework** for the "Measure â†’ Pattern" step. By logging peak and end data consistently, the system can identify which memory arcs produce the best long-term outcomes and replicate them.
