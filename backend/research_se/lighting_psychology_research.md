## 1. Light and Arousal

### 1.1 Light Intensity and the Sympathetic Nervous System

Light is a direct environmental stressor that activates the autonomic nervous system. The sympathetic division ("fight-or-flight") responds to bright light exposure with measurable physiological changes:

- **Cortisol modulation:** Bright light exposure (>10,000 lux) on the rising phase of the circadian cortisol rhythm produces a >50% immediate elevation in cortisol levels, driving alertness and wakefulness. This effect is time-of-day dependent — morning bright light elevates cortisol, while evening exposure can suppress it. (Leproult et al., 2001; Jung et al., 2010)
- **Melanopsin pathway:** Intrinsically photosensitive retinal ganglion cells (ipRGCs) containing melanopsin project directly to the locus coeruleus and other arousal centers. Short-wavelength (blue, ~470nm) light is most effective at suppressing melatonin and increasing subjective alertness, reaction time reduction, and EEG delta-theta power changes. (Lockley et al., 2006; Cajochen et al., 2005)
- **Heart rate and skin conductance:** Sudden increases in illuminance trigger the orienting response — a transient deceleration in heart rate (parasympathetic) followed by sympathetic activation markers (pupil dilation, increased skin conductance). This is the physiological basis of "attention capture" by lighting changes. (Sokolov, 1963; live performance studies)

**Operational implication:** A lighting operator can directly manipulate crowd arousal through intensity. A sudden brightness increase is not merely visual — it is a neuroendocrine event.

### 1.2 Darkness and Social Disinhibition

Darkness in venues operates through multiple psychological mechanisms:

- **Anonymity effect:** Reduced visibility lowers the perceived risk of social judgment. When individuals feel less observable, self-consciousness decreases and behavioral inhibition weakens. This is the "mask of darkness" phenomenon — people act in ways they would not under full visibility. (Deindividuation theory; Zimbardo, 1969)
- **Deindividuation:** In dark, crowded environments, personal identity becomes submerged in the group. The combination of anonymity + crowd density + loud music produces a state where normative social constraints weaken. This explains why dark venues see more spontaneous dancing, physical proximity, and risk-taking behavior than equivalent bright spaces.
- **Safety paradox:** Research from Deakin University (2025) found that for each one-point increase in perceived venue brightness (on a 1-10 scale), the odds of experiencing sexual harm fell by 27%. Darkness increases both desired disinhibition (dancing, socializing) and undesired behavior (harassment, aggression). The operator must calibrate darkness as a tool, not a default.

**Operational implication:** Darkness is a behavioral amplifier — it magnifies whatever psychological state the crowd is already in. Use it with intent.

### 1.3 Sudden Brightness Change (Flash) and Attention

A sudden flash of light triggers the **orienting response** — an involuntary shift of attention toward the stimulus source. Neurophysiologically:

- The locus coeruleus-noradrenergic system releases norepinephrine, producing a transient state of heightened attention
- Pupillary light reflex (parasympathetic) causes constriction, while the orienting response (sympathetic) produces arousal
- The combination creates a "reset" moment where the crowd's collective attention reorients toward the lit area

In performance contexts, a flash at the exact moment of a musical drop or a performer's entrance creates a synchronized attention shift across the entire crowd. This is not coincidence — it is neurophysiological choreography.

**Operational implication:** Use sudden brightness changes as punctuation marks. They work best when the crowd is already in a state of anticipation.

### 1.4 Optimal Base Lighting Levels by Crowd State

| Crowd State | Recommended Illuminance | Rationale |
|-------------|------------------------|-----------|
| **Social / Arrival** | 50-150 lux (warm, dim) | Allows facial recognition at close range without exposing the full room. Supports conversation and social scanning. |
| **Transitional / Warming** | 20-80 lux (coloured wash) | Reduces visibility enough to encourage movement toward the dancefloor while maintaining enough light for navigation. |
| **Peak / High Energy** | 5-30 lux (punctuated by flashes) | Minimal base light keeps focus on the light show itself. Darkness between flashes extends the visual impact of each flash. |
| **Wind-down / Recovery** | 30-100 lux (warm, rising) | Gradual increase signals the shift from peak to social state. Warm colour temperature supports parasympathetic recovery. |

*Note: These are operational ranges, not absolute values. Venue size, ceiling height, and fixture output will require calibration.*

## 2. Colour Psychology in Live Environments

### 2.1 Major Colours — Operational Profiles

#### Red (620-750nm)
- **Psychological effect:** Arousal, urgency, aggression, passion. Red is the most physiologically activating colour. It increases heart rate, blood pressure, and perceived temperature. In live environments, it signals danger, desire, or peak intensity.
- **When to use:** Build-ups to drops, high-energy genres (techno, hardstyle), moments requiring unified crowd intensity. Red washes during peak hours create a visceral, almost aggressive energy.
- **When to avoid:** Early in the night (overwhelming), during wind-down (prevents relaxation), in contexts where aggression is a risk (certain crowd demographics, alcohol-heavy environments). Red also has the highest seizure-triggering potential in strobe contexts.
- **Operational note:** Deep reds feel heavy and oppressive; quick red accents feel urgent. Use saturation to control the emotional weight.

#### Blue (450-495nm)
- **Psychological effect:** Calm, cool, space, distance. Blue light suppresses melatonin less than shorter wavelengths but still maintains alertness. It creates perceived "space" and "depth" — the room feels larger.
- **Specific crowd effect:** Blue slows perceived tempo. In festival settings, cooler coloured stage lighting was associated with increased glare complaints, suggesting blue at high intensity can become uncomfortable rather than calming. (Live performance studies, Portugal festival data)
- **When to use:** Chill-out zones, deep house/ambient sets, transitional moments, creating mystery or "underwater" atmosphere.
- **When to avoid:** Peak energy moments (deflates intensity), as front light on performers (washes out skin tones, creates cold/unapproachable feel).
- **Operational note:** Pale blue soothes; deep blue hints at secrecy or melancholy. Pair with white for clean vocal moments.

#### Green (495-570nm)
- **Psychological effect:** Neutral, natural, balanced. Green is the least physiologically activating major colour. It sits in the middle of the visible spectrum and produces minimal arousal response.
- **Specific crowd effect:** Provides visual "rest" between high-intensity colour moments. Green can feel "fresh" or "renewing" — a palate cleanser for the visual system.
- **When to use:** Recovery moments between peaks, nature-themed events, creating balance in a multi-colour sequence. Good for gobo textures (foliage, organic patterns).
- **When to avoid:** As primary front light on performers (can make skin look sickly or unnatural — the "witch light" effect).
- **Operational note:** Mint/leaf greens read gentle; dark green can tilt eerie. Use textured gobos for forest/garden looks.

#### Amber / Warm White (2700K-3000K)
- **Psychological effect:** Comfort, intimacy, social warmth. Amber triggers associations with firelight, sunset, and safety. It is the most "human" colour temperature.
- **Specific crowd effect:** Encourages social interaction, conversation, and physical closeness. Warm light makes spaces feel smaller and more enclosed — psychologically "cozier." Studies show happy songs are rated most positively under warm-white LEDs, while the same music scores lowest under blue light.
- **When to use:** Arrival/early evening, VIP lounges, acoustic/singer-songwriter moments, wind-down phases, any moment requiring emotional connection.
- **When to avoid:** Peak electronic moments (insufficient energy), large outdoor stages (gets lost in ambient light).
- **Operational note:** Soft amber flatters skin tones. Sharp amber/yellow pops for upbeat hits. This is your "social glue" colour.

#### Purple / Violet (380-450nm)
- **Psychological effect:** Euphoria, mystique, luxury, creativity. Purple combines the arousal of red with the depth of blue. It occupies a unique position — stimulating enough for dancefloors, mysterious enough for immersive environments.
- **Specific crowd effect:** Purple creates a sense of "otherworldliness" or "fantasy." It is strongly associated with EDM and trance culture. Lavender reads dreamy; plum reads dramatic.
- **When to use:** Peak euphoric moments, VIP sections, fantasy/themed events, moments requiring emotional depth without aggression.
- **When to avoid:** Situations requiring clarity or focus (purple scatters in the eye, reducing visual acuity).
- **Operational note:** Purple is your "emotional peak" colour — use it when you want the crowd to feel transcendent rather than aggressive.

#### White / Full Spectrum (4000K-6500K)
- **Psychological effect:** Harsh, alert, clinical, peak. Full-spectrum white contains all wavelengths and produces maximum visual information. It is the most "honest" light — it reveals everything.
- **Specific crowd effect:** White light maximizes alertness and visual acuity. It can feel "exposing" — the opposite of darkness. In performance, a white flash creates maximum contrast against coloured environments.
- **When to use:** Peak climax moments, strobe effects, revealing the full room ("house lights up" for encore calls), moments requiring maximum visibility.
- **When to avoid:** Extended use (causes visual fatigue, feels clinical), intimate/social moments (destroys atmosphere).
- **Operational note:** White is your "nuclear option" — highest impact, fastest fatigue. Use sparingly and for short duration.

### 2.2 Colour Saturation

| Saturation Level | Crowd Response | Operational Use |
|------------------|----------------|-----------------|
| **Highly saturated** | Intense, immersive, emotionally charged | Peak moments, genre-specific identity (techno red, trance purple), high-energy drops |
| **Pastel / low saturation** | Soft, dreamy, approachable | Early evening, romantic moments, transitions, creating atmosphere without demanding attention |
| **Desaturated / near-white** | Neutral, functional, "reset" | Between colour changes, allowing the eye to recover, preparing for the next colour statement |

**Key insight:** The eye's cone receptors fatigue quickly under saturated colours. Alternating between saturated and desaturated states prevents visual fatigue and maintains the impact of each colour choice.

### 2.3 Colour Temperature — Warm vs Cool

| Temperature | Range | Social Effect | Best Use |
|-------------|-------|---------------|----------|
| **Warm** | 2200K-3000K | Intimacy, comfort, relaxation, social bonding | Lounges, arrival, wind-down, acoustic sets, VIP areas |
| **Neutral** | 3500K-4100K | Balance, naturalness, adaptability | Multi-purpose spaces, transition zones, food service areas |
| **Cool** | 5000K-6500K | Alertness, focus, energy, perceived cleanliness | Task areas, restrooms, exit routes, moments requiring visual clarity |

**Operational insight:** Warm light (2700K) makes people feel socially safe and inclined to linger. Cool light (6500K) makes people feel alert and can increase perceived anxiety in social contexts. In nightlife, cool light works for "modern/urban" aesthetics but can feel clinical if overused. The shift from warm to cool over the course of a night can mirror the shift from social to peak energy states.

## 3. Strobe and Flash Effects

### 3.1 The Neuroscience of Strobe — Neural Oscillation Entrainment

Stroboscopic light produces **steady-state visually evoked potentials (SSVEPs)** — rhythmic electrical responses in the visual cortex that match the frequency of the flickering stimulus. Key findings:

- **40 Hz strobe** induces strong gamma-band (30-80 Hz) neural entrainment in the visual cortex and beyond (hippocampus, prefrontal cortex). This synchronization enhances functional connectivity between brain regions. (Herrmann, 2001; Multiple EEG studies)
- **Entrainment strength** is directly proportional to the perceived intensity of the flicker. Stroboscopic light produces significantly stronger entrainment than invisible spectral flicker at the same average brightness. (Wittevrongel et al., 2022)
- **Neural consequence:** When strobe frequency aligns with the brain's natural gamma oscillations (~40 Hz), it creates a state of heightened neural synchrony. This feels subjectively like "being in the zone" or "losing yourself in the music" — it is literally a brain state of enhanced inter-regional communication.

### 3.2 Safe Strobe Frequencies and Seizure Risk

| Frequency Range | Risk Level | Operational Guidance |
|-----------------|------------|----------------------|
| **1-4 Hz** | Minimal risk | Safe for general use. HSE (UK) recommends maximum 4 Hz for public performances. |
| **4-10 Hz** | Low-moderate risk | Use with caution. Some individuals may experience discomfort. |
| **10-25 Hz** | **Highest risk** | **Primary seizure-triggering range.** Avoid sustained use. This is the danger zone for photosensitive epilepsy. |
| **25-30 Hz** | Moderate risk | Risk decreases above 25 Hz but individual sensitivity varies. |
| **30-60 Hz** | Lower risk | Faster flicker becomes less perceptible. Still avoid if audience seizure risk is a concern. |
| **>60 Hz** | Minimal perceptible risk | Generally not perceived as flicker. Safe but loses strobe effect. |

**Critical safety data:**
- Photosensitive epilepsy affects ~3-5% of people with epilepsy (more common in ages 7-19)
- Red light is more likely to provoke seizures than blue or white
- Sustained exposure >0.5 seconds at trigger frequencies increases risk
- The Pokémon incident (1997) involved 12.5 Hz red-blue flashing at full screen for 4 seconds, hospitalizing ~700 children

**Operational rule:** Never sustain strobe in the 10-25 Hz range. Use it only as brief punctuation (<2 seconds), and never as a continuous effect.

### 3.3 Strobe-BPM Alignment and Entrainment Amplification

When strobe frequency is mathematically related to music BPM, entrainment is amplified:

| Music BPM | Quarter-note Strobe (Hz) | Eighth-note Strobe (Hz) | Sixteenth-note Strobe (Hz) |
|-----------|--------------------------|-------------------------|---------------------------|
| 120 BPM | 2 Hz | 4 Hz | 8 Hz |
| 128 BPM | 2.13 Hz | 4.27 Hz | 8.53 Hz |
| 130 BPM | 2.17 Hz | 4.33 Hz | 8.67 Hz |
| 140 BPM | 2.33 Hz | 4.67 Hz | 9.33 Hz |
| 150 BPM | 2.5 Hz | 5 Hz | 10 Hz |

**Operational principle:**
- **Quarter-note strobe (BPM/60):** Creates a slow, dramatic pulse. Best for build-ups and breakdowns.
- **Eighth-note strobe (BPM/30):** The "sweet spot" for most electronic music. Creates visible rhythm without discomfort. Aligns with safe frequency ranges.
- **Sixteenth-note strobe (BPM/15):** Fast, energetic. Approaches the lower risk threshold. Use for short bursts only.

**Why alignment works:** The brain's motor system naturally entrains to rhythmic auditory stimuli. When visual flicker is phase-locked to the same rhythm, auditory and visual entrainment reinforce each other, producing stronger collective synchrony. This is why a well-timed strobe on the drop "hits harder" — it is not just visual; it is a multisensory entrainment event.

### 3.4 Strobe as Transition Tool

Strobe is most powerful as a **transition marker** rather than a continuous effect:

| Transition Type | Strobe Application | Effect |
|-----------------|-------------------|--------|
| **Build-up to drop** | Accelerating strobe frequency from 1 Hz to 4 Hz over 8 bars | Creates rising tension, signals imminent peak |
| **Drop moment** | Single bright flash or 2-3 rapid flashes at drop hit | Punctuates the musical moment, creates shared attention |
| **Tempo shift** | Strobe at old BPM → blackout → strobe at new BPM | Marks the boundary between musical sections |
| **Genre transition** | Strobe burst → colour change → new movement pattern | Resets visual expectation, prepares crowd for new energy |

**Operational rule:** The most impactful strobe is the one that stops. A flash followed by darkness creates more impact than continuous flashing. Use strobe as exclamation points, not commas.

### 3.5 The "Freeze Frame" Effect

At high strobe frequencies (>10 Hz), the brain begins to perceive motion as a series of discrete static images rather than continuous movement. This is the **stroboscopic effect** or "freeze frame":

- **Mechanism:** Each flash "freezes" the visual scene at that moment. Between flashes, the scene is dark. The brain stitches these frozen moments together.
- **Dancefloor impact:** Dancers appear to move in a series of poses rather than fluid motion. This creates a surreal, "matrix-like" visual experience.
- **Optimal frequency:** 8-15 Hz produces the most pronounced freeze-frame effect while remaining visually coherent. Above 20 Hz, the effect begins to blur back into continuous motion.
- **Operational use:** Use during peak moments for visual spectacle. The freeze-frame makes ordinary movement look extraordinary. Pair with fog/haze to make light beams visible between freezes.

## 4. Light Movement Speed

### 4.1 Slow Moving Lights (Sweeps, Fades)

| Parameter | Effect | Behavioral Output |
|-----------|--------|-------------------|
| **Speed** | < 10°/second pan/tilt | Calming, hypnotic, ambient |
| **Movement type** | Wide sweeps, slow fades, gentle colour transitions | Reduces physiological arousal, encourages swaying rather than active dancing |
| **Psychological effect** | Contemplative, immersive, "floating" | Supports introspection, conversation, and low-energy socializing |
| **Best use** | Early evening, ambient sets, breakdowns, wind-down | Creates "breathing room" in the visual environment |

**Neurological basis:** Slow, predictable movement does not trigger the orienting response. The visual system can track the motion without attentional shifts, allowing the brain to remain in a lower arousal state. Slow movement is processed by the magnocellular pathway (motion detection) without triggering the parvocellular attention networks.

### 4.2 Fast Moving Lights (Chase, Rapid Scan)

| Parameter | Effect | Behavioral Output |
|-----------|--------|-------------------|
| **Speed** | > 30°/second pan/tilt | Exciting, urgent, stimulating |
| **Movement type** | Rapid chases, quick scans, snap changes | Increases physiological arousal, encourages active movement |
| **Psychological effect** | Energizing, "wired," urgent | Supports high-energy dancing, jumping, moshing |
| **Best use** | Peak hours, drops, high-BPM genres, build-ups | Creates visual "adrenaline" matching musical intensity |

**Neurological basis:** Fast, unpredictable movement triggers repeated orienting responses. Each direction change forces attentional reallocation, maintaining a state of heightened arousal. The brain cannot habituate to unpredictable fast movement, so the arousal effect is sustained.

### 4.3 Synchronised Light Movement with BPM

**Does beat-locked lighting amplify entrainment? Yes.**

Research on movement entrainment in concert settings shows that:
- Musical features of pulse clarity, high spectral flux, percussiveness, and bass frequencies evoke more movement
- When visual movement is phase-locked to the same pulse, auditory and visual entrainment converge, producing stronger collective synchrony
- Synchronized light movement creates a "super-stimulus" where multiple sensory channels reinforce the same rhythmic structure

**Operational implementation:**
- Program moving heads to pan/tilt on beat (quarter-note or eighth-note)
- Use colour changes on downbeats
- Coordinate strobe with kick drum
- Time gobo rotations with musical phrases (every 4 or 8 bars)

**Why it works:** The brain's motor cortex is primed by auditory rhythm. Visual rhythm that aligns with auditory rhythm does not compete for attention — it is integrated into the same predictive model. The crowd "feels" the lights as part of the music.

### 4.4 When to Desynchronise

Desynchronization is a powerful contrast tool:

| Scenario | Desynchronization Technique | Effect |
|----------|---------------------------|--------|
| **Breakdown** | Lights move half-time while music builds | Creates tension through visual-auditory mismatch |
| **Drop surprise** | Lights hold static while music drops, then explode into motion | The contrast makes the subsequent motion feel more intense |
| **Genre contrast** | Lights move at old BPM while music shifts to new BPM | Signals transition before the crowd consciously registers it |
| **Chaos moment** | Random, unsynchronized movement during peak energy | Creates "controlled chaos" — overwhelming in a pleasurable way |

**Operational principle:** Synchronization creates comfort and predictability. Desynchronization creates tension and surprise. Alternate between the two to maintain dynamic range in the visual experience.

## 5. Darkness and Shadow

### 5.1 The Psychological Function of Darkness

Darkness in venues serves three primary psychological functions:

1. **Disinhibition:** As visibility decreases, social monitoring decreases. People feel less observed and therefore less constrained by social norms. This is the primary driver of "dance like nobody's watching" behavior.

2. **Intimacy:** Darkness creates psychological proximity. In a dark room, the effective "social distance" between strangers collapses. People stand closer, speak more softly, and engage in more physical contact.

3. **Anonymity:** Darkness provides a "mask" that separates behavior from identity. Actions in darkness feel less consequential because they are less attributable. This is why dark venues see more spontaneous behavior than lit ones.

**The "mask of darkness" quote:** *"Give a man a mask and they will show you who they truly are."* Darkness is that mask.

### 5.2 Zones of Darkness and Light — Crowd Distribution

Lighting creates **behavioral territories** within a venue:

| Zone Type | Lighting Characteristic | Crowd Behavior |
|-----------|------------------------|----------------|
| **Bright zones** | High illuminance, white/warm light | Social interaction, conversation, visibility-seeking, "safe" behavior |
| **Dim zones** | Low illuminance, coloured wash | Transitional behavior, movement between zones, exploratory behavior |
| **Dark zones** | Near-black, minimal light | High disinhibition, dancing, physical proximity, risk-taking |
| **Spotlit zones** | Focused beam on specific area | Crowd movement toward light, attention convergence, "stage" behavior |

**Operational insight:** You can direct crowd flow by manipulating light zones. Want people to move toward the bar? Light the bar. Want to clear the dancefloor? Light it brightly with white. Want to concentrate energy? Darken everything except the dancefloor.

### 5.3 The Spotlight Effect

Illuminating a specific area creates an automatic attentional and behavioral pull:

- **Attention:** The human visual system is drawn to the brightest point in the visual field (luminance contrast principle)
- **Movement:** People physically move toward illuminated areas (positive phototaxis, modified by social context)
- **Behavior:** The lit area becomes a "stage" — people in it perform more, aware they are visible

**Operational applications:**
- Spotlight the DJ booth to establish it as the visual anchor
- Use moving spotlights to "lead" the crowd's attention across the room
- Create "islands of light" in dark zones to create intimate sub-groups
- Use spotlight on the dancefloor during key moments to create a "stage" for the crowd itself

### 5.4 Darkness as Recovery

After peak stimulation, the nervous system requires recovery time:

- **Sensory overload:** Extended exposure to bright, flashing, fast-moving light produces visual fatigue and habituation. The impact of each subsequent stimulus decreases.
- **Darkness reset:** A period of darkness (10-30 seconds) allows the visual system to recover sensitivity. Photoreceptors regenerate, and the orienting response "resets."
- **Physiological recovery:** Darkness triggers parasympathetic activation (rest-and-digest), reducing heart rate and cortisol. This is why a dark moment after a peak feels like "catching your breath."

**Operational protocol:** After every major peak (drop, strobe sequence, bright flash), insert a brief darkness moment. This is not "dead time" — it is recovery time that makes the next peak more impactful.

## 6. Operational Tables

### 6.1 Crowd State → Lighting State Matrix

| Crowd State | Intensity | Colour | Movement Speed | Strobe | Darkness |
|-------------|-----------|--------|----------------|--------|----------|
| **Arrival / Social** | 50-150 lux | Amber, warm white (2700K) | Static or very slow (breathing fades) | None | Minimal — room should be navigable |
| **Warm-up / Transitional** | 20-80 lux | Soft colours (pastel blue, green, pink) | Slow sweeps (5-10°/s) | Occasional 1-2 Hz pulse | Moderate — encourage movement toward floor |
| **Building / Rising** | 10-50 lux | Saturated colours (red, purple) building intensity | Medium speed (15-25°/s), beat-synced | Accelerating 1→4 Hz | Increasing — tension through contrast |
| **Peak / Maximum** | 5-20 lux base + bright flashes | Full saturation (red, white, purple) | Fast (30-60°/s), chaotic | 4-8 Hz bursts on drops | Maximum — flashes against black |
| **Release / Euphoric** | 20-60 lux | Purple, gold, warm white | Medium, flowing | Slowing 4→1 Hz | Moderate — "afterglow" lighting |
| **Wind-down / Recovery** | 30-100 lux, gradually rising | Warm amber, soft white (2700-3000K) | Slow to static | None | Minimal — return to visibility |
| **Last Call / Exit** | 100-200 lux | Warm white, rising colour temp | Static | None | None — full visibility for safety |

### 6.2 Lighting Transitions — Energy Arc Reinforcement vs Destruction

| Transition Type | Reinforces Energy Arc? | Why | Example |
|-----------------|----------------------|-----|---------|
| **Gradual intensity increase over 16+ bars** | Yes | Builds anticipation through progressive revelation | Slow fade from dark to red wash |
| **Sudden blackout → bright flash** | Yes | Creates contrast and surprise — the "reset" effect | Blackout for 2 beats, white flash on drop |
| **Colour shift warm → cool** | Yes | Signals energy increase (warm = social, cool = peak) | Amber to blue-white during build-up |
| **Slow movement → fast movement** | Yes | Mirrors musical energy arc | Gentle sweeps to rapid chases |
| **Gradual intensity decrease** | Yes (in wind-down) | Signals closure and recovery | Slow fade to amber over 5 minutes |
| **Abrupt return to bright white** | No (during peak) | Destroys atmosphere, feels like "house lights up" | White wash during peak set — kills mood |
| **Random colour changes without musical logic** | No | Creates cognitive dissonance, breaks immersion | Purple → green → red with no musical reason |
| **Continuous strobe >30 seconds** | No | Causes visual fatigue, habituation, discomfort | Sustained 10 Hz strobe — crowd looks away |
| **Static lighting for >5 minutes** | No | Allows habituation, reduces arousal | Unchanging red wash — crowd becomes numb |
| **Darkness without subsequent light** | No (if prolonged) | Creates anxiety, confusion, safety concerns | Blackout >60 seconds — crowd becomes uneasy |

### 6.3 Lighting Prescriptions by Time-of-Night

| Time | Crowd Psychology | Recommended Lighting State | Key Fixtures | Avoid |
|------|-----------------|---------------------------|--------------|-------|
| **21:00-22:30** (Arrival) | Social, scanning, conversation | Warm wash (50-100 lux), static or slow movement, amber/2700K | Wash lights, uplights, booth light | Strobe, fast movement, cool colours, high intensity |
| **22:30-23:30** (Warm-up) | Transitioning, exploratory, first dancers | Dim coloured wash (20-50 lux), slow movement, pastel colours | Moving heads (slow), LED bars, haze | Bright white, rapid changes, sustained darkness |
| **23:30-01:00** (Build) | Anticipation, rising energy, floor filling | Saturated colours (red, purple), medium speed, beat-synced movement, occasional strobe | Moving heads, beam fixtures, strobes (brief) | Static lighting, cool colours, excessive brightness |
| **01:00-02:30** (Peak) | Maximum energy, immersion, collective ecstasy | Minimal base light (5-15 lux), fast chaotic movement, full saturation, strobe on drops, maximum darkness between flashes | All fixtures at maximum dynamic range | Bright base light, static scenes, warm colours, sustained strobe |
| **02:30-03:30** (Release) | Euphoric, communal, "afterglow" | Rising light (20-40 lux), purple/gold, flowing movement, slowing strobe | Wash lights, moving heads (flowing), matrix effects | Abrupt darkness, aggressive colours, fast cuts |
| **03:30-05:00** (Wind-down) | Fatigue, intimacy, conversation returning | Warm wash (40-80 lux), slow/static, amber/3000K, rising visibility | Wash lights, uplights, minimal movement | Strobe, fast movement, cool colours, bright flashes |
| **05:00+** (Exit) | Departure, safety, reality re-entry | Full visibility (100+ lux), warm white, static | House lights, emergency lighting, exit lighting | Darkness, coloured light, effects |
